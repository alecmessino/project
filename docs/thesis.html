Driftwood · thesis & findings

Trend-following, judged by the drawdown it avoids — not the return it promises.

Driftwood is a trend-following signal system for equities and ETFs (with a crypto sibling). It bets that price trends persist: an instrument drifting far relative to its own volatility tends to keep drifting. It does not forecast prices and it does not place trades — it flags and sizes trends, and reports the result honestly.

The bet

Across a universe of region- and factor-tilted ETFs — US, developed-international and emerging markets, crossed with value, growth, size, momentum, quality and min-vol — Driftwood measures, for each fund, how large its recent move is relative to its own noise (a volatility-normalized trend z-score), confirms direction with a channel breakout, sizes the position to a constant volatility budget, and only acts when the expected edge clears the transaction cost. It runs both time-series (each ETF on its own trend) and cross-sectional (long the strongest funds, short the weakest) — and can neutralize within region or factor to separate which style is leading from which region is leading.

The name

In the standard model of how a price moves — geometric Brownian motion, dS = μS·dt + σS·dW — there are exactly two forces. One is drift (the μ term): the deterministic, directional component — the expected return, the part that actually goes somewhere. The other is diffusion (the σ·dW term): undirected noise that averages to nothing. Every price path is a tug-of-war between them. Trend-following is, almost literally, the attempt to detect and harvest the drift while refusing to be fooled by the diffusion — which is why the core signal is simply “how large is the move relative to the noise that could have produced it?” The system is named for the quantity it targets.

Driftwood carries the metaphor the rest of the way. Driftwood does not swim — it is carried, shaped and moved by the prevailing current, going where the water is already going. That is precisely the trend-follower’s posture: don’t predict, don’t fight the tide; identify the drift and let it do the work. It is patient and cumulative, too — driftwood is the product of a long journey, weathered into its shape over time, the way the strategy only pays when a trend persists long enough to matter.

It is also a deliberate foil to its sibling system, mrbet, which trades mean reversion — the bet that a move snaps back. Mean reversion swims against the current; Driftwood goes with it. The two names encode opposite theses about the same data.

The research

The two effects are among the best-documented anomalies in finance: time-series momentum (Moskowitz, Ooi & Pedersen, 2012) and cross-sectional momentum (Jegadeesh & Titman, 1993), with the practitioner lineage in managed-futures “trend.” But the claim worth testing isn’t “momentum prints money.” It is that trend-following delivers convexity: it cuts exposure into sustained declines, trading away some upside for a fundamentally different drawdown profile. That — not raw return — is the pitch, and it is what the ETF backtests below put to the test.

Honesty

The model’s continuation parameter is a fitted-but-not-deeply-validated default; momentum is a public, partly-arbitraged anomaly; the equity windows are short relative to the claims; and this is a signal system, not investment advice. The figures are engineering validation, not expected returns. Past performance does not predict future results.
