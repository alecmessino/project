Driftwood · approach & methodology

Own the drift. Refuse the leakage.

The sextant — the instrument of precise measurement. Here it stands for the discipline the firm runs on: measure the after-tax dollars before committing a thing.

Driftwood is a registered investment adviser. The product is an after-tax architecture: two return engines — a tax-managed Structural Alpha book and a return-seeking Core Alpha model — routed by one tax-location engine into a single after-tax portfolio. Structural Alpha is engineered beta, not market timing: deliberate factor exposure (small/value, emerging markets, international, held on purpose through low-cost Avantis funds) run through active, mechanical tax management — lot protection, tax-loss harvesting, and asset location. We model the after-tax dollars first and show them before you commit.

What we deliver — Structural Alpha

Two edges, and we are precise about what each one is — and is not.

Engineered beta — deliberate factor exposure

We hold small-cap value, emerging-markets, and international exposure on purpose, expressed as low-cost Avantis holdings in the firm's allocation models — a risk-premia exposure choice. This is not a forecast that these funds out-perform the market. It is a deliberate, diversified structure: far less US-mega-cap-concentrated than a cap-weighted "global" index, by construction rather than by a tactical call.

Mechanical tax management — the part we quantify

The measurable edge is structural and deterministic: tax-loss harvesting, lot- and holding-period management that converts short-term gains into long-term, asset location across taxable / Traditional / Roth, and a lower blended fund cost. A 40-year after-tax study shows the tax-managed implementation keeps roughly twice the after-tax fraction of a tax-naive book of the same exposure. The Tax Lab computes this — your Estimated Structural Alpha — on your own bracket and state, and labels it for exactly what it is: illustrative tax + fee modeling, not a performance promise.

The architecture — two engines, one tax plan

The real product isn't either engine; it's the architecture that decides where each one belongs. Two return engines feed one tax-location engine and, from there, a single after-tax portfolio. Structural Alpha — diversified, low-turnover, tax-managed — is built for taxable wealth. Core Alpha — tactical, higher-turnover, research-intensive — is built for tax-advantaged capital, where its turnover costs nothing after tax. Same household, different accounts; the routing is the part that is hard to copy.

Core Alpha — the tactical engine

Core Alpha is the return-seeking sleeve, and where the name comes from: like driftwood carried by Lake Michigan's current, it goes with the prevailing move rather than fighting it. The signal is a single, well-documented quantity — a volatility-normalized trend score: the cumulative return over a lookback divided by the noise that could have produced it (a z-score under a random-walk null). It rewards persistent drift and ignores undirected diffusion.

Selection & sizing

Each month the engine ranks the whole region × size × style ETF universe by that score and holds the strongest half — long-only, always fully invested, inverse-volatility weighted so a quiet drifter earns more notional than a wild one. Monthly rebalancing keeps turnover (and the cost it carries) low, and a trade is taken only when the expected edge clears the spread. Trend gates which sleeves to own; a deliberately diversified, risk-balanced construction — far less US-mega-cap-concentrated than a cap-weighted "global" index — does the rest. Scores can be demeaned within region or factor to isolate which style leads inside each region (or which region leads for a style), so the book takes only the specific exposure it intends.

Research it builds on

Driftwood is an engineering synthesis of well-documented, peer-reviewed results, not a novel claim. The selection engine is cross-sectional momentum; the allocation is mean-variance diversification with volatility-normalized cross-sectional trend over a diversified universe (no factor tilt). The primary sources:

  • Markowitz, H. (1952). Portfolio Selection. Journal of Finance 7(1). — diversification and the risk–return trade-off; the basis for holding a broad region/size/style matrix and weighting by risk rather than by dollars.
  • Jegadeesh, N. & Titman, S. (1993). Returns to Buying Winners and Selling Losers. Journal of Finance 48(1). — cross-sectional (relative-strength) momentum: the rank-and-hold-the-strongest selection rule at the core of the book.
  • Fama, E. & French, K. (1992, 1993). The Cross-Section of Expected Stock Returns / Common Risk Factors in Stock and Bond Returns. Journal of Finance / Journal of Financial Economics. — the value and size premia the diversified structure holds on purpose as deliberate exposure (engineered beta), not a tactical bet.
  • Asness, C., Moskowitz, T. & Pedersen, L. (2013). Value and Momentum Everywhere. Journal of Finance 68(3). — value and momentum as complementary, pervasive premia across markets and regions, including international and emerging — the context for a book that spans the full region/size/style matrix.
  • Moskowitz, T., Ooi, Y. H. & Pedersen, L. (2012). Time Series Momentum. Journal of Financial Economics 104(2). — the volatility-scaled trend signal and constant-volatility sizing the system uses to measure and act on drift.
Honesty.
