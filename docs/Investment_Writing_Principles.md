# Investment Writing Principles

*The constitution for everything Driftwood publishes. Companion to `Editorial_Style_Guide.md` (voice),
`Content_Hierarchy.md` (where each page's ideas and numbers belong), and `Evidence_Register.md` (which
facts exist and why). When a draft and this document disagree, fix the draft.*

Insights is not a blog. It is a permanent body of writing, in the lineage of Howard Marks's memos, Morgan
Housel, Farnam Street, and the Berkshire letters. We publish rarely, and only what will still be true in
ten years. Internally these are essays; **in public copy we never call them "essays"** — the page presents
the title and nothing more.

---

## The governing rule

> **Do not optimize for launch. Optimize for permanence.** Every page, diagram, essay, and phrase should
> feel like it could be published unchanged ten years from now. If a decision improves short-term marketing
> but weakens long-term intellectual credibility, choose credibility every time. Driftwood is building an
> enduring body of work, not a content calendar.

Its two corollaries:

- **Driftwood publishes because something is *true*, not because something *happened*.**
- **Never publish anything whose primary purpose is client acquisition.** Ironically, the pieces that earn
  clients are the ones written for the reader, not for the pipeline. Readers can tell a publication from a
  brochure.

---

## What we do

- **Teach investing, not Driftwood.** The reader came to understand their money, not to read about a firm.
- **Never begin with the firm.** Open on an idea the reader already half-feels; let the firm arrive, if at
  all, as the natural conclusion.
- **Show before telling.** A picture of the mechanism beats an adjective about it.
- **Prefer one unforgettable idea over ten good ones.** A piece is about a single thing. If it needs two,
  it is two pieces.
- **Every paragraph should earn the next.** Cut any that doesn't move the one idea forward.
- **Prefer ideas over opinions, and first principles over forecasts.** We explain how investing works, not
  what will happen next.
- **One sentence from every piece should be quotable.** Write toward the line a reader will repeat.
- **Every piece must leave the reader with one memorable mental model** they can repeat in conversation —
  *Two Returns*, *the Invisible Expense Ratio*, *the Attention Gap*. If a draft doesn't hand the reader a
  new way to *see* investing, it isn't finished. This is the single strongest test of whether a piece
  belongs in the Canon.
- **Every piece should introduce one piece of vocabulary** from (or into) the Driftwood Lexicon — the
  words that make the mental model repeatable.
- **End with reflection, not persuasion.** Close on a thought, never a pitch or a CTA.

## What we never do

- Never publish market commentary. No "what the Fed did," no "what the election means."
- Never respond to headlines, and never explain the news. If it will feel dated in twelve months, don't
  publish it.
- Never chase keywords, and never write because SEO wants another article.
- Never let a product name (Structural Alpha, Core Alpha, Tax Lab) lead a piece; the ideas lead, always.
- Never quote a firm performance figure inside a piece — that belongs to the research layer, under the
  Evidence Register.

## Canonical definitions

A firm's vocabulary is only as strong as its consistency. A term defined twice is a term defined never.
The definitions below are canonical: every page, essay, and tool *references* them and never restates or
re-scopes them.

- **Structural Alpha** is the cumulative benefit of implementation decisions — *not* market prediction —
  including asset location, tax-loss harvesting, withdrawal sequencing, lot management, and similar
  structural choices. It has one home for each job: named once on the homepage, **calculated** in the
  After-Tax Review, **explained** in How We Invest, and kept off The State Atlas (which is reference, and
  describes state-tax *impact*, not a product).

Named works — **The State Atlas**, *Every Portfolio Has Two Returns*, and the pieces of the Canon — are
capitalized and read as titles. The Atlas keeps its literal handle in nav and browser title ("State Tax
Atlas") for context and search; in prose it is always **The State Atlas**. See the Style Guide for the
full naming discipline.

## The tests every piece must pass before it ships

1. **The Housel test.** Would this be worth reading with Driftwood's name removed? If yes, keep it. If it
   only works *because* it's attached to a firm, it's a brochure — rewrite it.
2. **The consulting test.** If any passage sounds like consulting, rewrite it. If Morgan Housel could have
   written the sentence, that's a compliment.
3. **The ten-year test.** Read it as if it's ten years old. If it has aged, it wasn't evergreen enough to
   publish.
4. **The one-idea test.** State the piece's single idea in one sentence. If you can't, it isn't finished.

---

Cadence is deliberately slow. A small, permanent collection that people return to beats a large one they
scroll past. When in doubt, publish less, and make it last longer.
