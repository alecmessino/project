How Vermont taxes investors

Every state taxes investors differently. Here is how Vermont treats capital gains at the top rate, the marriage penalty, estate and inheritance tax at death, municipal-bond interest, the §1202 QSBS exclusion, and a harvested loss — a plain reference to the state's tax code.

Vermont taxes long-term gains at a top effective 8.75%; a state estate tax; common-law step-up (only half of jointly-held property).

Frequently asked — 7 on Vermont
How are capital gains taxed in Vermont?

Loss treatment conforms to federal: capital losses net against gains and carry forward. Top effective long-term rate 8.75%. Quirk: listed securities get only a $5K exclusion; a 3%-of-AGI floor.

Does Vermont have a state estate or inheritance tax?

State estate tax (paid by the estate): top rate ~16%, exemption ~$5M. Confirm the figure with counsel.

Is there a marriage penalty in Vermont?

Joint brackets widen for couples, but by less than 2× — a partial marriage penalty that bites on higher incomes.

How does Vermont tax municipal-bond interest?

Only in-state municipal-bond interest escapes state tax; bonds from other states are taxed. The classic in-state muni preference that rewards a home-state ladder.

Does Vermont follow the federal QSBS (§1202) exclusion?

Conforms to IRC §1202 — the federal qualified small business stock gain exclusion carries through to the state return.

What happens to a capital loss you carry forward in Vermont?

Capital losses carry forward under the federal Section 1212 rules — a harvested loss nets against gains and rolls forward until used.
