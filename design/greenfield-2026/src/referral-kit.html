A working relationship

Your advice is sound. It arrives into a system nobody is running.

You draft the trust, and it is never funded. You model the conversion, and the client's advisor sells the wrong lot in the same year. The work was right; the sequence was nobody's job. Driftwood exists to hold that seat, and to bring you into the room before the decision is made rather than after.

We do not compete with you. We coordinate around you, and we put your name on the matters that are yours.

Every finding we surface names an owner. When the owner is the attorney or the CPA, that is written down and routed to you, with the context and the modeling already done.

What we do
  • Read the household as one system: investments, taxes, estate, liquidity, risk, ownership
  • Model the decision before it is made, and share the assumptions
  • Carry every open matter in a standing register with a status and a named owner
  • Bring you the work with the analysis attached, in the year it matters
  • Manage the portfolio, tax-aware, when the client wants that too
What we never do
  • Give tax or legal advice, or opine on your work product
  • Move a client's tax or legal work to another firm
  • Pay or receive a referral fee of any kind
  • Contact your client about anything outside the agreed scope
  • Let a decision touch your area without telling you first
When a client is worth a fifteen-minute call
A business sale ahead
Entity structure, QSBS eligibility, and the gifting sequence all set years before closing.
A move between states
Residency reopens income, estate, and trust situs at once. Illinois to Texas is not a small question.
Concentrated stock or equity comp
Bracket management, charitable structures, and basis decisions that interact.
A trust drafted but unfunded
The most common gap we find, and the cheapest to close.
An inheritance or a death in the family
Portability elections are lost by silence, not by decision.
No one coordinating
The client has good advisors who have never spoken to each other.
Why sequence is the whole argument

One composite household, roughly $8,000,000, an Illinois estate above the state's $4,000,000 exclusion. Each specialist did good work alone: the attorney drafted an ILIT, the CPA built a conversion ladder, the insurance seat funded a policy. Nothing was wrong. Nothing was sequenced.

≈ $395,000

The difference between those decisions made in isolation and the same decisions made in order. Not a clever instrument any one of us could have named alone.
