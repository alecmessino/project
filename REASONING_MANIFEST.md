# The Reasoning Manifest

*One page. The philosophical anchor for every Driftwood product. Read it before you build.*

---

Driftwood does not store pages.

Driftwood stores **facts**. Facts produce **reasoning**. Reasoning produces **decisions**. Products
render those decisions.

That sentence is the whole architecture, and it is the reason the firm looks the way it does.

**Facts are canonical.** A tax rate, an estate exemption, a step-up regime — each is authored once,
cited to its primary source, and stamped with the edition of law it reflects. It never lives in two
places, because two copies are two chances to be wrong, and a wealth institution cannot afford to be
casually wrong. When the law changes, we change one fact, and every surface that depends on it moves
with it. This is what makes Driftwood a reference rather than a blog.

**Reasoning exists once.** A fact on its own is inert — *Illinois exempts only $4M at death* is true and
useless until someone asks what it means for a household. So above the facts sits a graph of reasoning:
what the environment does to a household, how to weigh it, what it asks the household to coordinate, and
what should happen next. Each piece of that reasoning is a small, named, reusable object — not a
paragraph buried in a page. The estate-cliff signal is defined once; it reads the estate fact, it opens
a coordination priority, it carries its own citation. It is the same object whether it appears on a
state page, in a comparison, in a crossing brief, or in a household's record.

**Decisions are the point.** Everything the firm publishes is in service of a household deciding better.
Not "here is the rate in Texas," but "given this environment, here is what changes in your operating
system, and here is the sequence of things to do about it." Description is table stakes; the
coordination is the product. We would rather say one true, actionable thing than ten complete ones.

**Products render decisions — they do not own intelligence.** A state page, a two-state comparison, a
crossing brief, a household record, and one day an AI assistant are not five separate bodies of
knowledge. They are five *views* of the one reasoning graph. Each asks the graph a question —
*which signals differ? which actions change on the move? which priorities become this household's
standing decisions?* — and renders the answer well. None of them invents a rule. If a product needs new
intelligence, the intelligence goes in the graph, where every other product inherits it, not in the
product, where it rots in isolation.

This is why we do not hardcode.

Six months from now, someone will look at a product and say, reasonably, *why don't we just write this
copy directly on the page? It would be faster.* It would. And it would be the first crack. The second
page would say it slightly differently. The third would fall out of date when the law moved. Within a
year the firm would again be a pile of excellent, quietly-contradicting pages — exactly the thing this
architecture was built to escape. A product that owns its own intelligence is a liability with a nice
font.

So the discipline is simple, and it is worth defending:

> Facts are canonical and editioned.
> Reasoning is structured, reusable, and lives in one graph.
> Products are lightweight, well-made interfaces over that reasoning.
> Intelligence is never duplicated into a page.

Hold that line, and every new capability makes every existing one more valuable, because they all
compound the same underlying reasoning. Break it, and each new feature is just another island.

Driftwood is not a website with a lot of content. It is a small set of true facts, a graph that reasons
over them, and a growing family of products that render that reasoning where a household can act on it.

Build accordingly.
