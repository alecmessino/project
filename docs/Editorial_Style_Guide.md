# The Driftwood Editorial Style Guide

*How Driftwood writes. One voice, held steady across every page, as if written by the
same thoughtful person over many years.*

This guide exists so the firm reads as one mind. Before writing or editing any page,
read this. When a sentence and this guide disagree, change the sentence.

---

## The one idea

Everything on the site reinforces a single thought:

> **The world's best institutional investors focus on the decisions that compound after
> taxes. Driftwood brings that discipline to individual investors.**

If a page doesn't move a reader closer to understanding that idea, it is doing something
else, and probably shouldn't be there.

### The sentence the whole firm rests on

> *The largest investors in the world don't manage portfolios differently because they're
> smarter. They manage them differently because they pay attention to different things.*

Taxes. Asset location. Rebalancing. Diversification. Costs. Implementation. CPA
coordination. None of those are features. They are the things Driftwood pays attention
to — and the evidence that the idea above is real.

---

## Voice — five rules

**1. Teach investing, not Driftwood.**
The reader did not come to read about a firm. They came to feel understood about their
money. So the firm narrates; it is rarely the subject. Prefer the shape:

> Most investors assume… · Institutions do… · Individual investors usually… · Here's why…

over:

> We believe… · We built… · We offer… · Our platform…

The firm should almost disappear. Housel does this. Marks does this. Dimensional does
this. The writer becomes a clear-eyed narrator of how investing actually works, and
Driftwood is simply the one that behaves accordingly. *(One deliberate belief statement
as a headline is powerful — see rule 2. It is the constant drumbeat of "we / our / we
built" through the body that we cut.)*

**2. State the belief before the service.**
Lead with what is true, then explain, then — last, and briefly — say who does it.
Belief → explanation → positioning. Never open a page by describing what Driftwood sells.

**3. Institutional ideas, plain English.**
The reader knows taxes. They know they dislike Wall Street. They do not know what
"portfolio architecture" means, and they shouldn't have to. Say *asset location*, not
"tax-alpha routing." Say *what you keep*, not "after-tax optimization." The concept stays
sophisticated; the words get simpler.

**4. Evidence over adjectives.**
Cut *powerful, advanced, proprietary, innovative, cutting-edge, comprehensive, robust,
sophisticated, world-class, best-in-class, holistic, transformational.* They are claims
without proof. Replace a claim with the fact that earns it, or delete it. A number, a
mechanism, or a named source persuades; an adjective asks to be trusted.

**5. Philosophy is the headline; implementation is the proof.**
Make the belief the largest words on the page. Put the mechanism — asset location,
harvesting, the engines, the tools — underneath, as evidence that the belief is real.
Never lead with the machinery.

## Words we don't say → words we say

Run every page through this filter. Consistency is what turns a website into a brand.

| We don't say | We say |
| --- | --- |
| Research (as a value word) | Evidence |
| Optimization / optimize | Better decisions / portfolio design |
| Alpha engine / engine | Investment approach / how it's built |
| Framework | Philosophy / approach |
| Platform | Firm |
| Tool (as the point) | Resource |
| Quantitative | Systematic |
| Model (as a value word) | Evidence |
| Portfolio optimization | Portfolio design |
| Return maximization | After-tax outcomes / what you keep |
| Dashboard | Current portfolio |
| Signal | Trend |
| State map | State guide |
| Generate alpha | Improve what you keep |
| Client | Investor / family *(context dependent)* |
| Leverage (as a verb) | Use |
| Solution / offering | *(name the actual thing)* |

Two words earn special discipline:

- **"Architecture."** Powerful once, weak the twelfth time. Think of how Apple says
  "Retina display" — once, then shows it. Use *architecture* **three or four times across
  the entire site**, at moments that matter. Everywhere else: *design, structure, system,
  approach, portfolio construction, implementation, investment process.*
- **Product names — "Structural Alpha," "Core Alpha," "Tax Lab," "Leakage."** These are
  implementation details. A reader should understand the philosophy first, and discover
  the machinery only underneath it. Keep the names to the research, methodology, and tool
  pages where they belong; keep them out of the philosophy and the front door.

## Recurring themes (use them; don't overuse them)

- **After taxes is the only number that's real.** Pre-tax return is a headline; what you
  keep is the outcome.
- **A portfolio is one system.** Every account works together, not apart.
- **The controllable things compound.** Markets are uncertain; costs, taxes, and
  discipline are not.
- **Great portfolios are engineered, not assembled.** The one metaphor we lean on. Use it
  sparingly enough that it keeps its weight.

## The Driftwood Lexicon

The best firms invent language — not jargon, *compression*: a few words that hold a complex idea
and are easy to repeat. Over years these become "Driftwood language," more durable than any product
name. Use them precisely and consistently; introduce one, where it fits, in every published piece.
Define a term the first time a reader meets it, then trust it.

| Term | What it means |
| --- | --- |
| **Every Portfolio Has Two Returns** | The one it earns and the one you keep. The gap between them is where most wealth is won or lost. |
| **Portfolio Architecture** | The deliberate design of the whole — allocation, location, coordination — as opposed to a pile of good holdings. Rationed; see the "architecture" rule. |
| **Institutional Attention** | The habit of attending to what actually decides outcomes — cost, taxes, implementation, discipline, time — rather than to what is easy to market. |
| **The Attention Gap** | The distance between how institutions and individuals invest: not intelligence or access, but *what receives attention*. |
| **The Attention Stack** | The order attention should follow: asset allocation → asset location → taxes → estate → behavior → time. Each layer decides more than the holdings inside it. |
| **The Invisible Expense Ratio** | The unmeasured, unbilled drag of taxes, turnover, and reaction — never printed on a statement, paid a little each year. |
| **After-Tax Return** | The only return you ever spend. When we say "return" without qualification on client-facing pages, we mean this. |
| **Structural Friction** | Wealth lost to how a portfolio is built and run — the opposite of implementation done well. |
| **Compounding Interruptions** | Small, repeated leaks (a tax taken early, a panic sale) that break the chain compounding depends on. |
| **Decision Quality** | Judging a choice by the process and information behind it, not by a single year's outcome. |
| **One Portfolio. One System.** | Every account works together, not apart — allocation, location, harvesting, withdrawals, estate, coordinated as one. |
| **Stewardship** | *Internal concept only — not public positioning.* The care a great endowment takes with permanent capital. Keep the concrete "institutional portfolio architecture, built for individual investors" as the public line unless stewardship earns its way in through the writing over time. |

## Structure — how a page should open

1. **A belief**, stated plainly, in the largest type on the page.
2. **A short explanation** — teach the reader why it's true (institutions do X; most
   individuals don't; here's the cost of the gap).
3. **The positioning**, once, quietly: Driftwood brings the discipline large institutions
   use to individual investors.
4. **The proof** — the mechanism, the number, the tool — underneath, as evidence.

If a page can't be read top-to-bottom as *belief → why → who → proof*, it's ordered wrong.

## Canonical copy (use these verbatim, or don't drift far)

**Homepage opener**
> Most portfolios are built to maximize returns. We think they should be built to maximize
> what you actually keep.

**Positioning line (the "who," used sparingly)**
> Driftwood brings the same design principles used by large endowments, pension funds, and
> leading advisory firms to individual investors.

**The How-We-Invest thesis** *(sits above the principles; everything under it is proof)*
> Investing is complicated because markets are uncertain. Building a portfolio shouldn't
> be. Over decades, a handful of decisions consistently matter more than everything else:
> diversification, costs, taxes, discipline, and implementation. Our philosophy is simply
> an attempt to make those decisions deliberately instead of accidentally.

**The About close** *(the last thing a reader should feel)*
> Markets will always be uncertain. Costs are not. Taxes are not. Discipline is not. Those
> are the things we can control. They're also the things that compound.

## Editing rule: default to subtraction

When revising, cut before you add.

- If a sentence doesn't strengthen the firm's philosophy, remove it.
- If a paragraph can become one sentence, make it one sentence.
- If a concept can be shown rather than described, show it.
- Do not add a feature unless it materially improves understanding.

The goal is not a richer website. It is a more coherent firm — one people can still
describe accurately after they've closed the browser.

---

## Compliance — the lines that never change

Editorial freedom stops at the disclosures. These stay, verbatim, wherever they already
appear:

- "registered investment adviser," "Form ADV," "Form CRS," "adviserinfo.sec.gov."
- Every performance figure is hypothetical/illustrative and dated; past performance does
  not guarantee future results.
- Educational — not investment, tax, or legal advice.

Voice serves the reader; disclosure protects them. Both are non-negotiable.
