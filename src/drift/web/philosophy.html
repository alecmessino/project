The Driftwood Principles

How great portfolios are built.

Not by predicting markets. By building better systems.

I spent six years at Dimensional Fund Advisors, working alongside many of the country's largest advisory firms. The best investors I met didn't win by predicting markets. They won by building better systems.

Their conversations were never about which stock to buy. They were about how a portfolio should be structured, which assets belong in which accounts, and how to reduce taxes over decades — how to build something that keeps making good decisions without relying on forecasts.

Institutions have spent decades refining that discipline. Individual investors deserve the same thinking. That is why Driftwood exists.

The Driftwood principles

Markets are competitive.

The market already reflects an enormous amount of information. Consistently predicting short-term winners is extraordinarily difficult — so we don't build portfolios around forecasts.

Structure beats prediction.

The biggest driver of long-term results isn't finding the next great investment. It's owning a portfolio that keeps working across many different environments. Structure matters more than cleverness.

Taxes are an investment decision.

Every unnecessary tax dollar leaves the portfolio for good. Managing taxes isn't separate from investing. It is investing.

Diversification is intentional.

Diversification isn't owning a lot of things. It's deliberately combining different sources of risk and return. Good diversification is designed.

Discipline compounds.

Markets will always tempt you to react. The advantage comes from following a considered process instead of chasing headlines. Consistency compounds.

One portfolio. One system.

Asset allocation, asset location, tax-loss harvesting, withdrawal sequencing, concentrated stock, and estate coordination should not operate independently. They should work as one coordinated system.

Where this leads

Evidence-based investing — diversification, low cost, discipline — is well understood, and it is where we start. But it is only the starting point.

For a family paying taxes, holding a concentrated position, planning an estate, and drawing income across accounts, the harder and more valuable work is fitting those decisions together. Evidence-based investing is the starting point. Evidence-based wealth design is the destination.
