The Driftwood Principles

Five beliefs about money.

On markets, wealth, and time.

IReturn is not wealth.

An investment's return is what the market pays. Wealth is what a family keeps — after taxes, after costs, after mistakes, after time has done its work. The two are related, but they are not the same thing, and much of what goes wrong in investing comes from confusing them.

Chasing return is exciting. Keeping wealth is quiet. The industry mostly sells the first. Families mostly need the second.

IICompounding hates interruptions.

Money compounds the way a forest grows — slowly, invisibly, and only if nobody keeps digging it up. Most portfolios don't fail in crashes. They fail a little at a time: a tax bill that didn't need paying, a fee that never takes a year off, a sale made in fear that turned a bad season into a permanent loss.

None of these feel like decisions. All of them are.

IIITime is a family's edge.

Professionals are judged quarterly, so they behave quarterly. A family is judged over a lifetime — and is free to behave that way. That freedom is the one structural advantage an individual investor holds over the institutions, and it is the one most often thrown away.

Refusing to be hurried is not passivity. It is the edge.

IVBehavior is the multiplier.

Hand the same portfolio to two different temperaments and you will get two different fortunes. The plan matters less than the ability to stay with it in the years it looks wrong — and every good plan eventually looks wrong.

Calm is not a personality trait in investing. It is an asset.

VEnough is a number.

A portfolio exists to fund a life — the house, the tuitions, the decades after work, the things a family intends to leave behind. That means "enough" can be calculated, and once it can be calculated, risk stops being abstract. The question is never whether you beat an index. It is whether the life is funded, with room to spare.

These are the beliefs underneath everything Driftwood does. How they become an actual portfolio — the evidence, the taxes, the discipline — is set out in How We Invest. Why the firm exists at all is a shorter story, told in Our Story.
